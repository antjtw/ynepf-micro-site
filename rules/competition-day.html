How competition day runs

This explains how a competition day works: weighing in, the order you lift in, the clock, and choosing your weights.

This is a plain-English guide. The official IPF Technical Rules are the final word. For how our competitions run, see our FAQs.

Weigh-in

Weigh-in opens two hours before your group lifts, and lasts an hour and a half. You weigh in once, in a private room, with referees of your own sex. Bring photo ID.

If you are over or under your weight class, you can go back to the scales and try again, as long as you are still inside the weigh-in time. If you never make weight, you cannot win or place in that class.

At weigh-in you also give the weights for your first attempts. After weigh-in, you check your squat and bench rack heights and sign them off. That signed sheet is the official record if there is ever a dispute.

The round system

Everyone does their first squat, then everyone does their second, then their third. The competition then moves to the bench press, and last of all the deadlift. It always runs in this order.

Within each round, lifters go from the lightest weight to the heaviest. If two lifters pick the same weight, the one with the earlier lot number goes first.

If there are a lot of lifters, a class may be split into groups that lift at different times.

Choosing your weights

You get three attempts at each lift, and you choose the weight each time. A few rules shape those choices:

  • The weight on the bar is always a multiple of 2.5 kg.
  • You must go up by at least 2.5 kg between attempts.
  • The only time a smaller jump is allowed is for a record attempt, which must be at least 0.5 kg over the current record.

After each attempt, you have one minute to tell the table the weight you want next. You give this to a marshal, who passes it to the speaker.

The one-minute clock

When the bar is loaded and the speaker calls your name, a one-minute clock starts. You must begin your lift within that minute, or you lose the attempt.

This is why you check your rack heights early. Once the bar is announced as ready, the clock runs, and any last changes have to happen inside your minute.

If something goes wrong

Sometimes the bar is loaded to the wrong weight, or the speaker calls the wrong number. The chief referee decides what happens, and you will not be punished for someone else's mistake:

  • If the bar was lighter than you asked and you succeed, you can keep the lift or take it again at the right weight.
  • If the bar was heavier than you asked and you succeed, the lift counts.
  • If a loading error costs you a good lift, you get another attempt at the end of the round.

Other things to know

  • Three failed attempts at any lift takes you out of the overall competition, but you can still win awards on the lifts you completed.
  • Leave the platform within 30 seconds of finishing your attempt.
  • Tie your hair back so it does not block the referees' view.
  • Do not adjust your kit or use ammonia in view of the crowd. You may adjust your belt.

The people running the day

A team of officials keeps the competition moving:

  • Speaker — the MC. Calls each lifter, announces the weights, and keeps the day on track.
  • Timekeeper — runs the one-minute clock and other time limits.
  • Marshals — collect your next weight and pass it to the speaker.
  • Scorers — record every attempt and the results.
  • Spotters and loaders — load the bar, set up the bench, and keep you safe.
  • Technical controller — checks each lifter is correctly dressed before they lift.
