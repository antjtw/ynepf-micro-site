Referees and the lights

Three referees judge every lift. They decide if your lift is good or not, and show you with coloured lights.

This is a plain-English guide. The official IPF Technical Rules are the final word.

The three referees

One chief referee sits at the front. Two side referees sit at the sides of the platform. They sit where they can see your lift clearly, within about 4 metres.

The chief referee gives the spoken signals during the squat and bench press. The side referees do not speak, they just judge.

The lights

After you finish a lift, each referee shows a light:

  • White light means a good lift.
  • Red light means no lift.

You need at least two white lights for the lift to count. If your lift is a "no lift", the referees also show cards or paddles that tell you why it failed.

The signals for each lift

The referee's signals for the start and end of each lift
LiftTo startTo finish
Squat"Squat", with a downward arm movement"Rack", with a backward arm movement
Bench press"Start", with a downward arm movement, then "Press" once the bar is still on your chest"Rack", with a backward arm movement

What referees check before you lift

Before the competition starts, the referees make sure that:

  • the platform, bar and discs meet the rules
  • the scales are accurate
  • every lifter is inside their weight class
  • each lifter's kit has been checked

It is your job to get your kit checked before you lift. If you wear something that was not checked, you could lose your attempt.

Kit inspection

Before your group lifts, at least two referees check everyone's kit. Each item is examined and then stamped or marked to show it has passed. Wraps that are too long can be cut down and checked again. Anything dirty or torn is turned away.

A few things do not need checking: watches, jewellery, mouthpieces, eyewear and feminine hygiene items. You can have help putting your knee sleeves on, but not with sliding devices or lubricants.

The failure cards

When a lift is a "no lift", the referees raise a coloured card or paddle to show the reason. There are three colours:

  • Red — a problem with the depth or the bar's path, for example not reaching depth in the squat, or not lowering the bar to your chest in the bench press.
  • Blue — a problem with your body position, for example not locking out, bouncing, or any downward movement of the bar.
  • Yellow — a problem with your feet, the signals, or contact, for example stepping, missing a signal, or being helped by a spotter.

The jury

At bigger competitions a jury of senior referees watches over the session. Their job is to make sure the rules are applied correctly. They can step in if a clear mistake is made, and they can replace a referee who is not judging well.

Appeals

If you disagree with a decision, you (or your coach) can appeal to the jury. Do this straight away, as soon as the thing you are appealing about has happened. The jury may ask for the appeal in writing, and their decision is final.
